1. Draw histogram of the variable Books..how.many.per.year. What shape is your distribution? Report the appropriate central tendency measure and variability measure based on distribution shape. Justify your choice.

Histogram of Books Read per Year

hist(my.data$Books..how.many.per.year,
     main = "Histogram of Books Read per Year",
     xlab = "Number of Books",
     col = "lightblue",
     border = "black")

Summary Statistics

median_books <- median(my.data$Books..how.many.per.year, na.rm = TRUE)
iqr_books    <- IQR(my.data$Books..how.many.per.year, na.rm = TRUE)

cat("Median:", median_books, "\n")
## Median: 7
cat("IQR:", iqr_books, "\n")
## IQR: 11

Conclusion

The histogram of Books..how.many.per.year is right-skewed and asymmetric, with most students reading between 0 and 10 books, while a few read many more, creating a long right tail. Because the distribution is asymmetric, the median (7 books) is a better measure of central tendency than the mean, and the IQR (11 books) better describes the variability by focusing on the middle 50% of the data, unaffected by the extreme values.

2. Draw boxplot for the variable Friends.on.Facebook. Report all the crucial values from the graph and interpret them. Do you see any outliers? Explain the term.

Boxplot of Friends.on.Facebook

boxplot(my.data$Friends.on.Facebook,
        main = "Boxplot of Friends on Facebook",
        ylab = "Number of Friends",
        col = "lightgreen",
        border = "darkgreen")

summary_friends <- summary(my.data$Friends.on.Facebook)

cat("Summary statistics for Friends.on.Facebook:\n")
## Summary statistics for Friends.on.Facebook:
print(summary_friends)
##    Min. 1st Qu.  Median    Mean 3rd Qu.    Max.    NA's 
##     0.0   150.0   300.0   417.1   487.5  5000.0      55
cat("IQR:", iqr_friends, "\n")
## IQR: 337.5

The boxplot displays:

  • The median (the central line inside the box) represents the 50th percentile; half of the observations have a lower number of friends, and half have a higher number.
  • The lower quartile (Q1) and upper quartile (Q3) (the edges of the box) indicate that 25% of the data falls below Q1 and 25% is above Q3.
  • The IQR is the difference between Q3 and Q1, showing the range of the middle 50% of the data.
  • The whiskers extend to the most extreme data points within 1.5 * IQR from the quartiles. These points beyond the whiskers are considered outliers.

An outlier is an observation that is significantly higher or lower than the rest of the data. Outliers can indicate variability in the data, errors in data collection, or exceptions in this case for example individuals with an exceptionally high or low number of Facebook friends compared to most of the sample.

x <- 9                   
n <- 10 * x + 5          
p <- 1/37                
z <- 4                   

prob_z <- dbinom(z, size = n, prob = p)
# P(X < 5) = P(X <= 4)
prob_less_than_5 <- pbinom(4, size = n, prob = p)

# Round probabilities to two decimals
prob_z_r <- round(prob_z, 2)
prob_less_than_5_r <- round(prob_less_than_5, 2)

PMF Plot

# A sequence of possible wins from 0 to n
k_values <- 0:n

# PMF values: P(X = k)
pmf_values <- dbinom(k_values, size = n, prob = p)

plot(k_values, pmf_values,
     type = "h",  
     main = paste("PMF of Binomial(", n, ",", round(p,4), ")"),
     xlab = "Number of Wins (k)",
     ylab = "P(X = k)",
     col = "blue",
     lwd = 2)

CDF Plot

# CDF values: P(X <= k)
cdf_values <- pbinom(k_values, size = n, prob = p)
